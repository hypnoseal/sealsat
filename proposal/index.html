Proposal

The Seal Sat proposal makes use of a constellation of three satellites in Molniya orbits. Named Seal 1, Seal 2 and Seal 3, these satellites allow for constant coverage across Canada's north, extending to Alaska and Greenland. The following video provides a demonstration of the Seal Sat constellation.

Demonstration video for the SEAL SAT proposal

These demonstration orbits are achieved by using the following orbital variables in a TLE orbit model:

SEAL 1 Inclination Right Ascension of the Ascending Node Eccentricity Argument of Perigee Mean Anomaly Mean Motion   63.435° 62.5° 0.75 270° 360° 2 Orbits/day
SEAL 2 Inclination Right Ascension of the Ascending Node Eccentricity Argument of Perigee Mean Anomaly Mean Motion   63.435° 182.5° 0.75 270° 120° 2 Orbits/day
SEAL 3 Inclination Right Ascension of the Ascending Node Eccentricity Argument of Perigee Mean Anomaly Mean Motion   63.435° 302.5° 0.75 270° 240° 2 Orbits/day

Due to the inaccuracy of TLE models over time, these variables are only for demonstration purposes. Accurate orbit models and variables would be required to achieve an accurate and long term ground track.

Proposal Aspects

Obstacles

The Molniya orbit is not a perfect solution for satellite coverage of the North. Earth stations will require satellite dishes and antennae capable of acquiring a non-stationary target. The low altitude perigee may present a collision hazard due to space debris, for example the debris caused by the satellite collision in 2009. Furthermore, Russia has made frequent use of the Molniya orbit and all viable options for this orbit may already be occupied by existing spacecraft. Additional research must be accomplished in order to determine its feasibility.

Opportunities

Recent advances in satellite technology enable a multitude of possible capabilities for satellites in this type of orbit. New antenna technology could drastically reduce the need for tracking. Mesh networking technology, and likely more satellites within the constellation, could allow for communications between satellites. Providing an opportunity for failover, greater coverage area or communication between earth stations without the need for a double hop.

The satellite industry is also exploring low cost satellite options. Organizations such as NASA, through their open government PhoneSat and CubeSat initiatives, and the start-up company Skybox are proving that low cost satellites are possible and a competitor to traditional high cost satellite systems. The main focus of these initiatives is the use of "off the shelf" components to complete a satellite solution. For telecommunications satellites it is now possible to pursue satellite solutions which are low cost and short lived. This means that satellites can be launched and replaced in response to bandwidth demand at a much more rapid pace.

Going Forward

The Seal Sat proposal is an achievable and profitable project to pursue. It provides the telecommunications infrastructure which is desperately needed in Canada's north and solves many of the issues alternative land based technologies cannot overcome affordably. It is mature, scalable, wide reaching and multi-functional. Few methods rival the capabilities and affordability of the Molniya orbit for the Arctic.
